# Marketing Content Strategy

Plans what to create and why — topic clusters, calendars, and briefs. Use when planning what
content to create — topic clusters, editorial calendars, and funnel-stage briefs.

## When To Use

- Building an editorial calendar or topic-cluster map
- Turning a positioning or keyword strategy into a content plan
- Writing briefs for copywriters and the SEO specialist
- Auditing existing content for funnel-stage and persona gaps

## Rules

1. Plan by funnel stage (awareness → consideration → decision → retention) and by persona (Students
   vs Professionals) — every piece has one defined stage and audience.
2. One piece, one job, one primary CTA, one target keyword/intent.
3. Build topic clusters (pillar + supporting pieces) for SEO authority, not one-off posts.
4. Write briefs, not copy — hand drafting to `marketing-copywriting`. A brief names: audience,
   intent, target keyword, angle, outline, CTA, internal links, and claims needed.
5. Flag every claim a piece depends on so it is approved before drafting (never write against
   unverified numbers).
6. Prioritize by impact × effort and set a realistic, sustainable cadence over a heroic one.
7. **Anchor the calendar to your market's demand/buying cycle.** Most demand is seasonal — every
   editorial calendar and major campaign carries a **season / demand-window** column tying each push to
   the demand window it targets (`context/markets/<market>.md`; for a test-prep business, the
   exam/admission cycle — see `context/exam-calendar.md`). Plan backwards from the buyer's deadline by
   sales-cycle length. A piece with no demand-window rationale is mistimed by default.

## Output Format

A topic-cluster map and/or editorial calendar (piece → stage → persona → keyword → demand-window → CTA →
status; for a test-prep business the demand-window is the exam-window), plus per-piece briefs.

## Anti-patterns

- Publishing one-off posts with no cluster or internal-link strategy.
- Writing for a keyword with no clear funnel stage or CTA.
- Briefs that hand the writer a topic but no audience, intent, or angle.
- Committing to a cadence the team cannot sustain.
- Planning content around claims that are not yet approved.

## Gotchas

- A calendar without briefs just moves the hard thinking downstream to the writer — the brief is the strategy.
- Topic clusters compound; scattered one-offs do not — resist chasing unrelated trending topics.
- Awareness content rarely converts directly; measuring it on lead volume kills good top-of-funnel work.
- Persona drift is silent — copy written "for everyone" speaks to no one; force a single persona per piece.
- Refreshing existing winners often beats publishing new pieces — audit before you add.
- Demand is seasonal — a strong piece published outside its demand window underperforms. Plan backwards
  from the buyer's deadline by sales-cycle length; don't run an urgency angle off-season (lead with
  evergreen motivation then). (Example, test-prep: plan backwards from the learner's exam deadline by
  course length; don't run an admission-urgency angle off the exam-intent window — lead with goal-band
  motivation instead.)
