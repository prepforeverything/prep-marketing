# Marketing Psychology

Scope notes: Use when selecting persuasion tactics, applying pricing psychology, choosing influence frameworks, or designing nudges. Provides 50+ mental models organized by marketing challenge with ethical guardrails.

Applies behavioral economics, cognitive biases, and influence frameworks to marketing decisions — model selection, pricing, conversion optimization, and nudge design.

## When To Use

- Selecting persuasion models for a campaign or conversion flow
- Understanding why buyers act or fail to act at a specific funnel stage
- Making pricing and packaging decisions where psychology affects perceived value
- Optimizing conversion rates by diagnosing friction or motivation gaps
- Designing nudge sequences that move prospects through decision stages

## Rules

1. Always tie model selection to the specific marketing challenge before browsing models — "which model fits this problem?" before "what models exist?"
2. Combine 2–3 models rather than relying on a single framework; behavior is multi-causal.
3. Never apply models without ethical consideration — dark patterns erode trust faster than they convert; short-term lift from deception produces long-term churn.
4. Check `marketing-product-context` for audience context before selecting models — loss aversion operates differently for enterprise B2B buyers versus impulse consumer purchases.
5. Validate model application against real customer language from context section 9, not internal assumptions about buyer motivation.
6. Use the quick-reference table in `references/mental-models.md` to narrow options by challenge before deep-diving into individual models.

## Anti-patterns

- Applying scarcity or urgency language when the offer is always available — detected fake urgency destroys credibility
- Using dark patterns: forced continuity, hidden costs, confirmshaming, roach motel flows
- Stacking too many influence techniques in one touchpoint — it reads as manipulation and increases cognitive load
- Choosing models based on novelty or personal interest rather than fit with the specific challenge at hand

## Gotchas

- Psychology models are descriptive, not prescriptive — they describe tendencies, not guarantees; A/B test before treating any model as settled
- Culture and context change how models apply — loss aversion does not operate the same way across enterprise B2B, consumer SaaS, and impulse e-commerce
- The same model can help or harm depending on how it is applied — reciprocity used with genuine generosity builds goodwill; reciprocity used manipulatively triggers reactance
- Ethical guardrails are non-negotiable: if a tactic works by deceiving the user, it fails the ethics test regardless of measured conversion lift

## Ethical Guardrails

- **Transparency:** the user should understand what they are getting and what it costs — no hidden fees, buried terms, or misleading framing
- **Reversibility:** make it easy to undo — unsubscribe, cancel, return — friction at exit is a trust signal, not a retention strategy
- **Proportionality:** the intensity of the nudge should be proportional to the weight of the decision — high-pressure tactics for low-stakes decisions backfire
- **Honesty:** all claims must be provable; social proof must reflect real customers; scarcity must be genuine; urgency must reflect an actual deadline
