# Marketing Positioning

## When To Use

- Defining or revisiting a product's market position
- Crafting a value proposition or differentiation statement
- Designing a new category or reframing an existing one
- Validating messaging with customer language

## Rules

1. Check `marketing-product-context` output before gathering context that may already exist.
2. Begin every positioning exercise with competitive alternatives analysis — what customers do or use without your product — not with internal strengths.
3. Identify unique attributes (what you have or do that alternatives do not), then map those attributes to meaningful customer outcomes before writing any messaging.
4. Identify best-fit customers from the value themes that emerged — positioning clarity comes from narrowing the audience, not broadening it.
5. Determine the market category that makes the product's value obvious. Category choice shapes buyer expectations before the first word of copy.
6. Apply JTBD Four Forces to positioning validation: address push (pain with current solution), pull (appeal of new solution), habit (inertia), and anxiety (risk of switching). Messaging that ignores any force leaves a switching barrier unaddressed.
7. Follow the messaging hierarchy in order: category claim → differentiating value → proof points → CTA. Each level must be earned before the next.
8. Validate positioning language using actual customer words from interviews, reviews, and support tickets. Internal language is not a substitute.
9. Differentiation must be demonstrable with evidence — claiming it in copy without proof is positioning theater.
10. Revisit positioning when the market shifts, a major competitor enters, or growth stalls unexpectedly.

## Anti-patterns

- Copying or mirroring competitor language instead of differentiating from it
- "Everything to everyone" positioning that dilutes the best-fit customer signal
- Feature-focused messaging that lists capabilities without mapping to customer outcomes
- Changing positioning based on internal opinion without supporting customer research
- Ignoring emotional and social job dimensions and addressing only functional jobs

## Gotchas

- Do not activate this skill for one-off copy edits — use it only when positioning strategy is in question and competitive alternatives analysis is needed.
- Competitive differentiation requires validated market knowledge; this skill surfaces the right questions but cannot validate claims against real market data.
- Overuse of superlatives ("best", "only", "fastest") in positioning copy will surface as unverified claims — provide evidence or soften them before finalizing.
- Positioning built on internal assumptions without customer language validation will feel right internally and fail in market.
- The Obviously Awesome five-step sequence requires completion in order — skipping competitive alternatives analysis and jumping to messaging produces undifferentiated copy.
